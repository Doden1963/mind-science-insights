🌀 Spiritual perspective

Spiritual perspective, PEAR-inspired experiment, PEAR-LAB, and preliminary interpretation.

Dansk See this section inside full text Back to category

In everything related to the spiritual, it may be worth remembering that even the wisest ape cannot learn to go into a supermarket and buy, for example, a watermelon.
If one imagines a direction of development from ape to human – and then further on in a kind of multidimensional direction – one may perhaps think that there are beings that actually exist and operate on a level that human beings simply cannot grasp or learn to understand.
In that context, one may also imagine that heaven is not merely a picture or an idea, but a truly existing dimension of existence. Perhaps it is only there that the human being can fully come to understand such higher forms of existence.

🔹 The purpose of an experiment 🔹
I am doing this experiment to challenge a viewpoint that I experience as both reductionist and ultimately destructive:
the idea that the human being is nothing more than its physical brain.
If this viewpoint were fully adequate, then:

• conscious intention should not be able to influence physical randomness

• human will should be fully derived from deterministic or random processes

• subjective experience should be a mere byproduct without causal power

My experiment is therefore an attempt to investigate whether consciousness can have an independent, measurable influence on physical processes — even when these are based on electronic noise and quantum fluctuations.
It is not about “proving” anything definitively,
but about testing whether reality behaves less mechanically than a purely materialistic worldview assumes.
A PEAR Lab–inspired experiment on consciousness, randomness, and physical noise.
Inspired by the pioneering experiments at the PEAR laboratory at Princeton University and related research, this project investigates a fundamental question:
Can human consciousness influence physical systems in a measurable way?
To investigate this, I have built a hardware-based noise generator that exploits physical random processes. The system is designed and assembled on a custom-made circuit board and placed in an enclosure. It continuously generates physical noise, which is processed and analyzed in real time.
On the software side, I have developed customized tools in Python for data acquisition, live visualization, and statistical analysis. The purpose is to test whether focused human intention can lead to deviations from randomness that exceed what one would statistically expect from chance alone.
The project does not assume a specific outcome. Instead, standardized statistical methods, controlled measurements, and transparent data logging are used to investigate whether reproducible and statistically significant effects can be observed.
What you see here is the current status of the experiment — hardware, software, live data, and ongoing development.

It is difficult to imagine how one could influence quantum events in an electronic circuit when one does not know at all what is happening moment by moment in voltages and currents. But who knows what a presence one may have on other planes of existence can do, or others there, hypothetically.
We may perhaps already be controlling quantum processes in the brain every day when we make decisions, solve open-ended problems, and do things that AI still cannot do – for example, driving a car from A to B.
To get faster and statistically stronger measurements, one might perhaps use many independent zener-noise generators and analyze their combined output. But it takes time to build that many circuits, especially when one also has a physically demanding full-time job.
Perhaps it makes the most sense simply to continue with the setup I already have – and try to capture something resembling what PEAR-LAB at Princeton said they saw. It just takes longer to collect enough data.
What I experienced actually resembles something PEAR-LAB also noticed.
⭐ What PEAR-LAB found in their 30-year noise experiments
Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research (PEAR) conducted experiments for nearly three decades in which completely ordinary people tried to influence random noise generators by intention alone.
The most striking thing was that the noise generators responded – but not always in the way the subject wanted.
Intention reversal
In many tests, something strange happened:

• When the operator tried to make the curve go up, it sometimes went down

• When the person tried to make it go down, it went up

• The effect did not look like simple noise – there were patterns, directions, and correlations

The researchers gave it a name: intention reversal – a phenomenon where the system responds, but in the opposite direction of conscious intention.
The effect was weak, but statistically strong
A single session rarely showed anything dramatic. But when thousands of sessions were combined, the deviation was consistent and so statistically strong that the probability that it was due to chance was calculated to be less than one in trillions.
Not pure noise – but a pattern
PEAR found effects such as:

• Autocorrelations in the noise

• Deviations that did not follow the normal distribution

• Correlations between the operator’s mental state and the results

• Experimenter effects (the person setting up the experiment influenced the result)

It behaved too “systematically” for pure chance to explain it.
Personal signatures
PEAR also found that:

• Some people always pulled the curve slightly upward

• Some always slightly downward

• And some had a stable tendency toward intention reversal

It was as if each person had a kind of “psychic signature” in the noise.
Distance and time made no difference
The effect also appeared when:

• The operator was in another building

• In another state

• On the other side of the USA

• Or even in experiments where the intention was given after the data had already been generated (retro-experiments)

No simple physical explanation
The setups were run in Faraday cages, electromagnetically shielded, tested against control sessions and different types of noise generators – but nothing technical could explain the patterns.
PEAR’s cautious conclusion was:

• There exists a form of systematic connection between human intention and physical randomness that is not easily explained by current physics or psychology.

What I myself saw in my measurement resembles a little what PEAR-LAB found:
A kind of response – just not necessarily in the direction one consciously tries.
And that makes it quite fascinating to investigate further.
🔹 Can the present change the past – without us noticing?
Some of the most controversial results from PEAR-LAB at Princeton pointed toward what is called retrocausality:
That intention in the present can influence the statistics in data that were already generated in the past.
It sounds like science fiction, but in several of their so-called retro-PK experiments, exactly such effects appeared:

👉 The statistical distribution of earlier bits changed slightly, but consistently.

If the past really can be influenced, should we not then experience time paradoxes?
For example:

• 1 bit in the past → triggers lightning

• 0 bit → no lightning

Lightning can change the weather → human behavior → who meets whom → who is born → DNA → the whole future
— a classic butterfly effect.
And yet we never experience our own present being “rewritten.”
Why?
⭐ Three of the most common interpretations

✔ 1) Self-consistency (the Novikov principle)

The most widespread interpretation is that the universe only allows retrocausal effects that do not create logical paradoxes.
If a change in the past would lead to an inconsistent present, then:

• the change does not happen

• or its consequences are neutralized

• or the effect remains statistically microscopic

The past can therefore only be “bent” within limits that preserve the existing timeline.
This fits well with PEAR-LAB’s observations:
Small, systematic deviations — but never macroscopic paradoxes.

✔ 2) Many-Worlds / time branches

In Everett’s many-worlds interpretation, every quantum possibility creates a new branch of reality.
If a retrocausal effect would lead to a paradox in one timeline:

• the change is realized in another branch

• the original branch remains unchanged

In the alternative branch:

• the lightning strikes

• the weather changes

• history develops differently

But you yourself experience only the branch in which your consciousness already exists.
Therefore, you never register that something “switches.”

✔ 3) No actual change of the past – only statistical shifts

A more conservative interpretation is that no concrete past events are changed, but only that there arise:

• weak correlations

• skews in probability distributions

• non-local connections without information paradoxes

In this interpretation, retrocausality is not a rewriting of history, but a time-symmetric coupling in which:

• present and past are part of one unified quantum description

• without any classical causal chain being broken

This is close to ideas from Aharonov, Cramer, and Huw Price.
⭐ Short conclusion
PEAR-LAB’s data can be interpreted as signs that:

• present and past are not completely independent

• but that the connection is strongly limited

Regardless of interpretation, the following holds:

✔ The effects are small

✔ No macroscopic paradoxes arise

✔ Experienced reality remains consistent

The past may possibly be influenced statistically,
but never in ways that break the coherent reality we experience.
🔹 My own preliminary interpretation
Personally, I lean most toward interpretation 3 —
that is, that there are no concrete rewritings of the past, but rather time-symmetric statistical shifts, where present and past are part of one unified quantum description.
But with a possible extension:

👉 That in certain free-choice events there may arise a limited number of temporary time-branches,

which diverge briefly —
but merge again when the consequences remain within a self-consistent framework.
In this model:

• Free choices open a small outcome space

• Several quantum possibilities may be realized temporarily

• The branches do not exist permanently as in classical many-worlds

• Only the courses that are mutually consistent survive and “recombine”

This avoids both:

• classical time paradoxes

• infinitely branching worlds

• and the need for macroscopic rewriting of history

At the same time, it preserves the possibility that consciousness and intention play an active role in which outcomes are stabilized.
This approach lies somewhere between:

• Aharonov’s time-symmetric quantum mechanics

• Cramer’s transactional interpretation

• and a strongly limited, recombining variant of many-worlds

At the same time, it is worth noting that all these well-known interpretations are probably incomplete,
since they exclusively describe phenomena within the observable physical plane and do not take into account
what may possibly take place in other layers or planes of existence of reality, if such exist.
— and overall, in my judgment, this model fits best with both PEAR-LAB’s observations and the complete lack of experienced time paradoxes in everyday life.
