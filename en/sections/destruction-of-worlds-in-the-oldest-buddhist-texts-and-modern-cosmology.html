🌌 Destruction of worlds in the oldest Buddhist texts – and modern cosmology

Transport between planes of existence, close textual reading, world dissolution, mahākappa, and heavens.

Dansk See this section inside full text Back to category

According to the earliest Buddhist scriptures (the Pāli canon), worlds perish in great cycles of contraction (samvatta).

• Oldest and most original mention: destruction by fire.

• Later expansions (still early, but not original): destruction by water and wind.

• The heavens above the affected level always survive.

According to the cosmic teaching (AN 7.62 and other texts):

• Fire → the world perishes up to the Ābhassara heaven.

☀️ The seven suns (Sattasūriya Sutta, AN 7.62)

• Small streams dry up.

• Larger rivers dry up.

• Lakes dry up.

• Even the Ganges dries up.

• The oceans evaporate.

• The earth begins to glow and smoke.

• The earth and Sineru burst into flames and are consumed without remainder.

The sutta describes only the dissolution of the physical world in this one world-system.
No death of beings is mentioned, which in the early Buddhist context means that beings are already reborn elsewhere in accordance with their karma.
The heavens are not mentioned in this sutta. It therefore does not describe a total annihilation of all planes of existence, but the dissolution of the physical world-structure and the planes of existence belonging to this one world-system.
This can be understood figuratively: not seven suns, but that the sun swells up — fully in line with modern astronomy about the sun’s development into a red giant.
