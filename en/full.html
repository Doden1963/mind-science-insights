🧭 Early Buddhism vs. Theravāda – and what the Buddha “really” said

It may be important to distinguish between early Buddhism and the Theravāda tradition, which is otherwise often regarded as the most original school.
Something has probably been changed since the Buddha said it – even in the earliest texts. Therefore, later additions may come from people who were less enlightened than the Buddha. At the same time, the Buddha’s enlightenment was not necessarily of the kind where he “always knew everything.”
According to his own statements, the Buddha had to direct his attention toward what he wanted to know; so he was not enlightened in the sense that he constantly knew everything.
Together with ChatGPT, I have tried to get closer to what the Buddha really said – and to the probability that he had genuine visions of the cosmos, parts of which science only discovered 2500 years later.

📚 Scholars: the Buddha probably used a simpler cosmology

The Buddha probably said only a few things about cosmology, according to scholars such as:

• Richard Gombrich (Oxford)

• Steven Collins (Chicago)

• Lambert Schmithausen (Hamburg)

• Rupert Gethin (Bristol)

• Bhikkhu Analayo (Lumbini & Hamburg)

They believe that the Buddha originally used a simple cosmology, and that the large cosmic systems in the canon were expanded later.
Arguments:

• in the earliest texts one encounters earthly and mythological motifs

• one world (the one we live in)

• known rivers such as the Ganges and Yamunā

• lakes from North Indian mythology

• and one world-mountain: Sineru

• the cosmology in the canon is inconsistent and layered

• large measurements and multiverse structures are probably later expansions

• the Buddha avoids speculative cosmology (e.g. MN 63)

🌞🌌 The few cosmological statements – and why the parallel is remarkable

But the few things the Buddha said as cosmology resemble the sun’s phase as a red giant star that engulfs the Earth, and Big Bounce cosmology.
Several scholars have pointed out that it is statistically surprising that precisely the few likely original cosmological statements lie so close to modern models, when the Buddha hardly spoke about cosmology at all.
If the Buddha merely used random ancient images, why do exactly these resemble modern astrophysics so much — while almost no other ancient cosmologies do?

• Because the Buddha said very little about cosmology, the parallel is remarkable and difficult to explain as pure coincidence.

Traditions and humanism

Cosmic world-axes, humanistic rankings, and Plato and the Buddha.

🌍 Cosmic world-axes in antiquity – ranked by humanism

In many ancient cultures, there is the idea of a cosmic structure (axis mundi) connecting planes of existence vertically – heaven, the human world, and the underworld.
But even though these structures resemble one another astonishingly, the traditions differ greatly in how humanistic they are: whether they place human suffering, dignity, and the possibility of liberation at the center – or whether cosmology is instead used to legitimize fate, hierarchy, and power.
Below, the most important traditions can therefore be ranked by degree of humanism.

🥇 Early Buddhism – Sineru / Meru (Nikāya tradition)

Humanism: very high

• No creator god

• No eternal soul

• No absolute hierarchy

• All beings can reach liberation

• Even heavenly realms are impermanent

The cosmos is not described to explain power, but to show that only Nibbāna is an exit from the whole cycle.
This is the most consistently humanistic cosmology in antiquity.

🏛️ Greek tradition – Olympus / Omphalos

Humanism: very low

• Gods above humans

• Fate rules everything

• No path of salvation

• Hierarchy is cosmically legitimized

Olympus represents a strongly hierarchical worldview, where gods stand above humans, and where fate governs both gods and mortals. There is no universal path to liberation and no abolition of suffering.
However, it is precisely in classical Greece that the first real humanism arises – not in mythology, but in philosophy.
With Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, the Stoics, and Epicurus, an ethics develops for the first time in which human reason, dignity, and inner freedom stand above the power of the gods.
Greek civilization is therefore one of the greatest cradles of humanism – precisely because it transcends its own cosmology.

👉 Overall point

👉 The most humanistic traditions arise either:

• where cosmology is dismantled (Greece)

• or where cosmology is subordinated to the ending of suffering (Buddhism)

This is a very deep pattern.
The less cosmology is used to legitimize power and hierarchy, the more it is used to relieve suffering and open a universal path to liberation.
Early Buddhism stands almost alone in antiquity in having:

• a fully developed cosmology

• without a creator god

• without hierarchy

• and with a universal exit from the entire cycle

and therefore appears as the most thoroughly humanistic cosmic tradition in antiquity.

🌌 Plato and the Buddha – two nearly contemporary paths to liberation

Around 400 BCE, two of the greatest thinkers in world history appear almost simultaneously:

• the Buddha in India

• Plato in Greece

They knew nothing of each other, yet independently developed some of the deepest theories about:

• suffering

• knowledge

• soul / consciousness

• liberation

• cosmic order

A comparison between them shows both striking similarities and decisive differences.

🧘 🥇 The Buddha – liberation from the whole cosmos

Humanism: very high
The Buddha’s starting point is existential:

• All conditioned things are suffering (dukkha)

• All is impermanent (anicca)

• No eternal soul (anattā)

The goal is not a better place in the cosmos, but an exit from the whole cycle of worlds.
Central features:

• No creator god

• No absolute metaphysical authority

• All beings can reach liberation

• Even gods are subject to impermanence

• Cosmos is secondary – the ending of suffering is primary

Nibbāna is not a heaven, but:

• cessation of craving

• cessation of rebirth

• cessation of cosmic bondage

➡️ The Buddha offers a universal, non-hierarchical path out of the entire system of existence.

🧠 🥈 Plato – the soul’s ascent through knowledge

Humanism: very high (philosophical)
Plato develops a cosmic hierarchy with:

• the sensory world (changing, imperfect)

• the world of forms (eternal, true, good)

The human being is a soul that has fallen into the body and must:

• know the truth

• purify itself through philosophy

• ascend toward the good

Central features:

• The soul is immortal

• The highest principle is “the Good” (above even the gods)

• Liberation happens through knowledge

• The philosopher can become free already in this life

But:

• The cosmos remains

• The soul continues as individual

• Hierarchy between souls and levels of knowledge

➡️ Plato offers an elitist, knowledge-based ascent within the cosmos.

Cosmology and planes of existence

Transport between planes of existence, close textual reading, world dissolution, mahākappa, and heavens.

🧘‍♂️ Sineru, planes of existence, and “transport” (cuti–paṭisandhi)

In early Buddhism, it is described that the Buddha and certain monks could move between planes of existence, not as physical journeys in space, but through states of consciousness attained in deep meditation.
The enormous measurements, ring mountains, and layered worlds came only in later cosmological systems.

👉 “In the oldest layers, Sineru is used to show that even the cosmic center in a world-system will one day perish – not because the whole universe ceases, but because all conditioned worlds are subject to impermanence.”

🌌 Cosmic worlds and “transport” between planes of existence in Buddhism

One may possibly infer from early Buddhism that the heavenly planes belonging to this world-system will be destroyed when the sun in a distant future engulfs the Earth and this whole cosmic system dissolves.
But at the same time, Buddhism teaches that there are countless other world-systems with their own planes of existence, which are not dissolving at the same time.
The question then is how beings are “transported” to these other worlds.
According to the oldest Buddhist texts, no physical journey through space and time occurs. The transition takes place through rebirth via consciousness and karma.

• When a being dies (cuti), a new consciousness (paṭisandhi) arises immediately in another plane of existence, determined by the state of mind and previous actions.

• No intermediate journey and no movement through space are described – only an immediate continuity of consciousness, like a flame lighting a new flame.

• Thus beings leave this world-system long before its physical dissolution and are reborn in other worlds in accordance with their karma.

One possible motivation for this thought is that both animals and humans solve complex open-ended problems every day that no one has yet taught artificial intelligence to solve in the same way without such an “antenna.”
Examples include:

• driving a car safely from A to B in unpredictable traffic situations

• using hands and arms to fold clothes neatly

• grasping objects with precise force and timing

• navigating intuitively in complex environments

These tasks require:

• continuous sensing

• rapid holistic understanding

• bodily coordination

• flexible problem-solving

and are often solved without conscious calculation.
It is therefore tempting to speculate that consciousness is not exclusively a closed system in the brain, but may also have access to a broader information field not yet fully understood by neuroscience or artificial intelligence.

🟢 The earliest ideas about planes of existence in Buddhism

According to modern scholars, the early Buddha taught that consciousness can be reborn in many different forms of existence – humans, animals, heavenly worlds, and worlds of suffering. These worlds were understood as real, but not eternal. No god judges – karma and the direction of the mind determine rebirth.
Even the highest heavenly realms are impermanent. Therefore, the Buddha pointed to one exit lying beyond all worlds: Nibbāna.

🔹 TEXTUAL CLARIFICATION – why beings are already reborn before Sineru burns up 🔹

• In the very earliest Buddhist texts (the Nikāya canon), world-dissolution (saṃvaṭṭa) is described as a slow, cosmic process, not as a sudden catastrophe.

🔥 What is described – and what is not?

📜 Aṅguttara Nikāya 7.62 – Sattasūriya Sutta (The Seven Suns)
Here it says:
“Ayaṃ mahāpathavī ca Sineru ca pabbatarājā
ādittā bhavissanti, sampajjalitā, ekā jalitā bhavissanti.”

• “This great earth and Sineru, king of mountains, will be ignited, blaze up, and become one burning mass.” 🔥

And further:
“Seyyathāpi sappi vā telaṃ vā anupādisesaṃ pariyādāya jhāyeyya…”

• “Just as clarified butter or oil burns completely without ash or soot.”

➡️ Crucial observation:

• ❌ Nothing is said about:

– sattā (beings) dying
– pāṇa (living creatures) perishing
– maraṇa (death) of humans, animals, or gods

📌 In Pāli texts, the death of beings is always mentioned explicitly when it is relevant.

👉 The fact that it is not mentioned here is therefore meaningful.

🧠 The textual conclusion

• When sattā (beings) are not mentioned while the whole physical structure is destroyed

• this means, according to a textual reading and modern research:

➡️ there are already no beings left in this world-system.

That is:

• punabbhava (rebirth) has already ceased locally

• beings have already died in their previous lives

• and have been reborn elsewhere in accordance with kamma (karma)

🔄 World-dissolution as process – not total annihilation
📜 Dīgha Nikāya 1 – Brahmajāla Sutta
“Saṃvaṭṭati loko, vivaṭṭati loko.”

• “The world contracts, the world expands.”

➡️ No element-mechanics are mentioned

➡️ No total annihilation of all life

➡️ Only gradual cessation of conditions

🌌 Other worlds exist at the same time

📜 Majjhima Nikāya 49 – Brahma-nimantanika Sutta
“Atthi kho, brahme, añño loko.”

• “There are other worlds, Brahmā.”

➡️ When one cakkavāḷa (world-system) ceases:

• others still exist

• beings are reborn there

• not through physical movement

• but through karmic rebirth (cuti–paṭisandhi)

🔄 Cosmic cycles without the end of life
📜 Majjhima Nikāya 4 – Bhayabherava Sutta
“Anekepi saṃvaṭṭavivaṭṭakappe anussarāmi.”

• “I recollect many aeons of world-contraction and expansion.”

➡️ Beings are reborn across these cycles

➡️ No cosmic pause is described in which all life ceases

🔥 Only fire in the earliest Buddhism

• In the Nikāya texts there is only:

– 🔥 destruction by fire (tejo-saṃvaṭṭa)

• ❌ No udaka-saṃvaṭṭa (water)

• ❌ No vāta-saṃvaṭṭa (wind)

➡️ Water and wind appear only in later Abhidhamma and commentarial traditions.

🧭 The crucial distinction

• 🔥 Sineru’s destruction:

– one local cakkavāḷa ceases

• 🌌 Not the whole universe

• 🧠 Sattā are already reborn elsewhere

• 🚫 No physical “evacuation”

• ✅ Only kamma and the direction of mind determine “where to”

🕊️ Only Nibbāna (nibbānaṃ) is the end of the whole cycle (saṃsāra).

✨ In short

• The fact that the death of beings is not mentioned means:

👉 they are already reborn elsewhere (punabbhava)

• Sineru burns later 🔥

• Life has already left the system 🌌

This is directly textual, documented in Pāli, and in accordance with modern research on the very earliest Buddhism.

🌌 Destruction of worlds in the oldest Buddhist texts – and modern cosmology

According to the earliest Buddhist scriptures (the Pāli canon), worlds perish in great cycles of contraction (samvatta).

• Oldest and most original mention: destruction by fire.

• Later expansions (still early, but not original): destruction by water and wind.

• The heavens above the affected level always survive.

According to the cosmic teaching (AN 7.62 and other texts):

• Fire → the world perishes up to the Ābhassara heaven.

☀️ The seven suns (Sattasūriya Sutta, AN 7.62)

• Small streams dry up.

• Larger rivers dry up.

• Lakes dry up.

• Even the Ganges dries up.

• The oceans evaporate.

• The earth begins to glow and smoke.

• The earth and Sineru burst into flames and are consumed without remainder.

The sutta describes only the dissolution of the physical world in this one world-system.
No death of beings is mentioned, which in the early Buddhist context means that beings are already reborn elsewhere in accordance with their karma.
The heavens are not mentioned in this sutta. It therefore does not describe a total annihilation of all planes of existence, but the dissolution of the physical world-structure and the planes of existence belonging to this one world-system.
This can be understood figuratively: not seven suns, but that the sun swells up — fully in line with modern astronomy about the sun’s development into a red giant.

🌍 Cosmological background + Mahākappa (4 phases)

The universe undergoes cycles of:

• expansion / unfolding (vivatta)

• duration

• contraction / destruction (samvatta)

• an empty period before a new unfolding

There are:

• thousandfold world-systems

• thousand-times-thousandfold

• and “great thousandfold world-systems”

One full cycle is called a mahākappa.
The Buddha said: If one had a mountain 14 km high and stroked it with a silk cloth once every 100 years, the mountain would wear away faster than a mahākappa would end.

🌌 1. What is a mahākappa?

In early Buddhism, a mahākappa (great aeon) is the longest conceivable cosmic unit of time.
A mahākappa consists of four immense periods, which together make up one full cosmic cycle:

• samvaṭṭa – dissolution of the world (contraction / destruction)

• samvaṭṭa-ṭṭhāyin – empty intermediate period after dissolution

• vivaṭṭa – re-arising / unfolding of the world (expansion)

• vivaṭṭa-ṭṭhāyin – stable period in which the world endures

Thus:

👉 A mahākappa = dissolution → emptiness → new unfolding → stable existence

Not just one contraction and one expansion – but also:

• a long empty phase without physical world

• and a long stable phase afterward

🧭 2. Is this only one universe or many?
Very importantly:

• A mahākappa applies to one world-system (one cakkavāḷa).

But there are:

• countless world-systems

• which are not necessarily in the same phase at the same time

So:

👉 When one world is dissolving

👉 others are in a stable phase

👉 and others are in a new unfolding

It is a genuine multiverse system.

🔥 3. World-dissolution is not a single event

In the texts, samvaṭṭa (dissolution) is described as:

• extremely slow

• stepwise

• happening over inconceivably long times

And it can happen in three ways (in later tradition):

• by fire (tejo-saṃvaṭṭa)

• by water (udaka-saṃvaṭṭa)

• by wind (vāta-saṃvaṭṭa)

But in the oldest layers, only fire is mentioned.
That means:

👉 Dissolution is not one catastrophe, but a long cosmic process.

🌌 4. Relation to Big Bounce cosmology

Big Bounce models typically have:

• expansion

• contraction

• bounce

• new expansion

But Buddhism has a richer structure:
1. stable world (vivaṭṭa-ṭṭhāyin)
2. dissolution (samvaṭṭa)
3. empty phase without world (samvaṭṭa-ṭṭhāyin)
4. new unfolding (vivaṭṭa)
→ then again stable phase
Thus:

👉 Not just bounce, but also a long “cosmic emptiness” between universes.

This closely resembles modern ideas of:

• “ekpyrotic phase”

• “pre-big-bang vacuum”

• “quantum vacuum state”

🧠 5. The Buddha says directly that he experienced many mahākappas

In e.g. MN 4 and MN 36 the Buddha says:
“I recollected many aeons of world-contraction
many aeons of world-expansion
many aeons of both movements…”
This is extremely important:
He does not merely say:

• many lives

but:

• many cosmic cycles

Thus:

👉 Samsara extends across countless universes through many mahākappas.

‘There I was with such a name, of such a clan, with such an appearance; this was my food, such was my experience of pleasure and pain, such was my lifespan; and having died there, I reappeared elsewhere; and there too I was with such a name, of such a clan, with such an appearance; this was my food, such was my experience of pleasure and pain, such was my lifespan; and having died there, I reappeared here.’
Thus I recollected my many past lives in their forms and details.
This was the first true knowledge I attained in the first watch of the night. Ignorance was dispelled, true knowledge arose, darkness was dispelled, light arose, as happens in one who lives vigilant, ardent, and resolute.
Once my concentrated mind was purified, bright, unblemished, rid of defilement, pliant, workable, steady, and imperturbable, I directed it to the knowledge of the passing away and reappearance of beings. With the divine eye, which is purified and surpasses the human, I saw beings passing away and reappearing, inferior and superior, beautiful and ugly, happily and unhappily reborn. I understood how beings continue according to their actions, thus:
‘These worthy beings, who lived badly in body, speech, and mind, reviled the noble ones, had wrong views, and let wrong views guide their actions, after the breakup of the body, after death, have reappeared in a state of misery, in a bad destination, in ruin, even in hell.
But these worthy beings, who lived well in body, speech, and mind, did not revile the noble ones, had right views, and let right views guide their actions, after the breakup of the body, after death, have reappeared in a good destination, even in the heavenly world.’
Thus with the divine eye, purified and surpassing the human, I saw beings passing away and reappearing, inferior and superior, beautiful and ugly, happily and unhappily reborn, and I understood how beings continue according to their actions.
This was the second true knowledge I attained in the middle watch of the night. Ignorance was dispelled, true knowledge arose, darkness was dispelled, light arose, as happens in one who lives vigilant, ardent, and resolute.
Once my concentrated mind was purified, bright, unblemished, rid of defilement, pliant, workable, steady, and imperturbable, I directed it to the knowledge of the destruction of the taints. I directly knew, as it really is:
‘This is suffering.’
‘This is the origin of suffering.’
‘This is the cessation of suffering.’
‘This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.’
I directly knew, as it really is:
‘These are the taints.’
‘This is the origin of the taints.’
‘This is the cessation of the taints.’
‘This is the path leading to the cessation of the taints.’
As I thus knew and saw, my mind was liberated from the taint of sensuality, from the taint of becoming, and from the taint of ignorance. When it was liberated, the knowledge arose: ‘It is liberated.’
I directly knew: ‘Birth is ended, the holy life has been lived, what had to be done has been done, there is no more returning to any state of being.’
This was the third true knowledge I attained in the last watch of the night. Ignorance was dispelled, true knowledge arose, darkness was dispelled, light arose, as happens in one who lives vigilant, ardent, and resolute.
Now, brahmin, it might occur to you: ‘Perhaps the ascetic Gotama is still not free today from desire, hatred, and delusion, and that is why he still seeks secluded resting places in the jungle thickets.’
But you should not think in that way. For it is because I see two benefits that I still seek secluded resting places in the jungle thickets: I see a pleasant abiding for myself here and now, and I have compassion for future generations.”

👼 Life in the heavens + the “fall” into a new world (vivatta)

👼 Life in the heavens is far better than life on Earth
The higher heavens (Ābhassara, Subhakiṇṇa, Vehapphala, and the arūpa worlds) are described as places with:

• no disease

• no hunger

• subtle or immaterial bodies

• jhāna bliss

• enormously long lives

• no conflicts

• great degrees of peace, light, and clarity

The Buddha says that even the highest earthly happiness cannot compare.

🔥 The fate of beings in the fire-destruction during cosmic contraction

• Humans, animals, hell-beings, and lower gods perish with their realm.

• Beings up to Ābhassara may rise to higher heavens or be reborn later.

• The highest heavens (above Vehapphala, including the arūpa worlds) always survive.

• Since there are countless world-systems, beings can be reborn in other universes not undergoing dissolution.

🌱 The beginning of a new world (vivatta) – the “fall”
When a world unfolds:

• The luminous Ābhassara devas come first.

• They are self-luminous, without sex, without gross bodies, without need for food.

• Some taste the earth’s nourishment → lose their light → the body becomes heavier.

• Differences arise → pride, envy, conflict.

This is the “fall” as in the Aggañña Sutta.

Experiment and retrocausality

Spiritual perspective, PEAR-inspired experiment, PEAR-LAB, and preliminary interpretation.

🌀 Spiritual perspective

In everything related to the spiritual, it may be worth remembering that even the wisest ape cannot learn to go into a supermarket and buy, for example, a watermelon.
If one imagines a direction of development from ape to human – and then further on in a kind of multidimensional direction – one may perhaps think that there are beings that actually exist and operate on a level that human beings simply cannot grasp or learn to understand.
In that context, one may also imagine that heaven is not merely a picture or an idea, but a truly existing dimension of existence. Perhaps it is only there that the human being can fully come to understand such higher forms of existence.

🔹 The purpose of an experiment 🔹
I am doing this experiment to challenge a viewpoint that I experience as both reductionist and ultimately destructive:
the idea that the human being is nothing more than its physical brain.
If this viewpoint were fully adequate, then:

• conscious intention should not be able to influence physical randomness

• human will should be fully derived from deterministic or random processes

• subjective experience should be a mere byproduct without causal power

My experiment is therefore an attempt to investigate whether consciousness can have an independent, measurable influence on physical processes — even when these are based on electronic noise and quantum fluctuations.
It is not about “proving” anything definitively,
but about testing whether reality behaves less mechanically than a purely materialistic worldview assumes.
A PEAR Lab–inspired experiment on consciousness, randomness, and physical noise.
Inspired by the pioneering experiments at the PEAR laboratory at Princeton University and related research, this project investigates a fundamental question:
Can human consciousness influence physical systems in a measurable way?
To investigate this, I have built a hardware-based noise generator that exploits physical random processes. The system is designed and assembled on a custom-made circuit board and placed in an enclosure. It continuously generates physical noise, which is processed and analyzed in real time.
On the software side, I have developed customized tools in Python for data acquisition, live visualization, and statistical analysis. The purpose is to test whether focused human intention can lead to deviations from randomness that exceed what one would statistically expect from chance alone.
The project does not assume a specific outcome. Instead, standardized statistical methods, controlled measurements, and transparent data logging are used to investigate whether reproducible and statistically significant effects can be observed.
What you see here is the current status of the experiment — hardware, software, live data, and ongoing development.

It is difficult to imagine how one could influence quantum events in an electronic circuit when one does not know at all what is happening moment by moment in voltages and currents. But who knows what a presence one may have on other planes of existence can do, or others there, hypothetically.

⭐ What PEAR-LAB found in their 30-year noise experiments
Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research (PEAR) conducted experiments for nearly three decades in which completely ordinary people tried to influence random noise generators by intention alone.
The most striking thing was that the noise generators responded – but not always in the way the subject wanted.
Intention reversal
In many tests, something strange happened:

• When the operator tried to make the curve go up, it sometimes went down

• When the person tried to make it go down, it went up

• The effect did not look like simple noise – there were patterns, directions, and correlations

The researchers gave it a name: intention reversal – a phenomenon where the system responds, but in the opposite direction of conscious intention.
The effect was weak, but statistically strong
A single session rarely showed anything dramatic. But when thousands of sessions were combined, the deviation was consistent and so statistically strong that the probability that it was due to chance was calculated to be less than one in trillions.
Not pure noise – but a pattern
PEAR found effects such as:

• Autocorrelations in the noise

• Deviations that did not follow the normal distribution

• Correlations between the operator’s mental state and the results

• Experimenter effects (the person setting up the experiment influenced the result)

It behaved too “systematically” for pure chance to explain it.
Personal signatures
PEAR also found that:

• Some people always pulled the curve slightly upward

• Some always slightly downward

• And some had a stable tendency toward intention reversal

It was as if each person had a kind of “psychic signature” in the noise.
Distance and time made no difference
The effect also appeared when:

• The operator was in another building

• In another state

• On the other side of the USA

• Or even in experiments where the intention was given after the data had already been generated (retro-experiments)

No simple physical explanation
The setups were run in Faraday cages, electromagnetically shielded, tested against control sessions and different types of noise generators – but nothing technical could explain the patterns.
PEAR’s cautious conclusion was:

• There exists a form of systematic connection between human intention and physical randomness that is not easily explained by current physics or psychology.

What I myself saw in my measurement resembles a little what PEAR-LAB found:
A kind of response – just not necessarily in the direction one consciously tries.
And that makes it quite fascinating to investigate further.
🔹 Can the present change the past – without us noticing?
Some of the most controversial results from PEAR-LAB at Princeton pointed toward what is called retrocausality:
That intention in the present can influence the statistics in data that were already generated in the past.
It sounds like science fiction, but in several of their so-called retro-PK experiments, exactly such effects appeared:

👉 The statistical distribution of earlier bits changed slightly, but consistently.

If the past really can be influenced, should we not then experience time paradoxes?
For example:

• 1 bit in the past → triggers lightning

• 0 bit → no lightning

Lightning can change the weather → human behavior → who meets whom → who is born → DNA → the whole future
— a classic butterfly effect.
And yet we never experience our own present being “rewritten.”
Why?
⭐ Three of the most common interpretations

✔ 1) Self-consistency (the Novikov principle)

The most widespread interpretation is that the universe only allows retrocausal effects that do not create logical paradoxes.
If a change in the past would lead to an inconsistent present, then:

• the change does not happen

• or its consequences are neutralized

• or the effect remains statistically microscopic

The past can therefore only be “bent” within limits that preserve the existing timeline.
This fits well with PEAR-LAB’s observations:
Small, systematic deviations — but never macroscopic paradoxes.

✔ 2) Many-Worlds / time branches

In Everett’s many-worlds interpretation, every quantum possibility creates a new branch of reality.
If a retrocausal effect would lead to a paradox in one timeline:

• the change is realized in another branch

• the original branch remains unchanged

In the alternative branch:

• the lightning strikes

• the weather changes

• history develops differently

But you yourself experience only the branch in which your consciousness already exists.
Therefore, you never register that something “switches.”

✔ 3) No actual change of the past – only statistical shifts

A more conservative interpretation is that no concrete past events are changed, but only that there arise:

• weak correlations

• skews in probability distributions

• non-local connections without information paradoxes

In this interpretation, retrocausality is not a rewriting of history, but a time-symmetric coupling in which:

• present and past are part of one unified quantum description

• without any classical causal chain being broken

This is close to ideas from Aharonov, Cramer, and Huw Price.
⭐ Short conclusion
PEAR-LAB’s data can be interpreted as signs that:

• present and past are not completely independent

• but that the connection is strongly limited

Regardless of interpretation, the following holds:

✔ The effects are small

✔ No macroscopic paradoxes arise

✔ Experienced reality remains consistent

The past may possibly be influenced statistically,
but never in ways that break the coherent reality we experience.
🔹 My own preliminary interpretation
Personally, I lean most toward interpretation 3 —
that is, that there are no concrete rewritings of the past, but rather time-symmetric statistical shifts, where present and past are part of one unified quantum description.
But with a possible extension:

👉 That in certain free-choice events there may arise a limited number of temporary time-branches,

which diverge briefly —
but merge again when the consequences remain within a self-consistent framework.
In this model:

• Free choices open a small outcome space

• Several quantum possibilities may be realized temporarily

• The branches do not exist permanently as in classical many-worlds

• Only the courses that are mutually consistent survive and “recombine”

This avoids both:

• classical time paradoxes

• infinitely branching worlds

• and the need for macroscopic rewriting of history

At the same time, it preserves the possibility that consciousness and intention play an active role in which outcomes are stabilized.
This approach lies somewhere between:

• Aharonov’s time-symmetric quantum mechanics

• Cramer’s transactional interpretation

• and a strongly limited, recombining variant of many-worlds

At the same time, it is worth noting that all these well-known interpretations are probably incomplete,
since they exclusively describe phenomena within the observable physical plane and do not take into account
what may possibly take place in other layers or planes of existence of reality, if such exist.
— and overall, in my judgment, this model fits best with both PEAR-LAB’s observations and the complete lack of experienced time paradoxes in everyday life.
