🧭 Early Buddhism vs. Theravāda – and what the Buddha “really” said

It may be important to distinguish between early Buddhism and the Theravāda tradition, which is otherwise often regarded as the most original school.

Something was probably changed after the Buddha said it – even in the earliest texts. Later additions may therefore come from people who were less enlightened than the Buddha. At the same time, the Buddha’s enlightenment was not necessarily of the kind where he “always knew everything.”

According to his own statements, the Buddha had to direct his attention toward what he wished to know about; in other words, he was not enlightened in the sense of constantly knowing everything.

Together with ChatGPT, I have tried to get closer to what the Buddha really said – and to the probability that he may have had real visions of the cosmos, parts of which science only discovered about 2,500 years later.

📚 Scholars: the Buddha probably used a simpler cosmology

The Buddha probably said only a few things about cosmology, according to scholars such as:

  • Richard Gombrich (Oxford)
  • Steven Collins (Chicago)
  • Lambert Schmithausen (Hamburg)
  • Rupert Gethin (Bristol)
  • Bhikkhu Analayo (Lumbini & Hamburg)

They believe that the Buddha originally used a simple cosmology, and that the large cosmic systems in the canon were expanded later.

Arguments:

  • the earliest texts are earthbound and mythological in a very limited way
  • one world (the one we live in)
  • known rivers such as the Ganges and Yamunā
  • lakes from North Indian mythology
  • and one world mountain: Sineru
  • the cosmology in the canon is inconsistent and layered
  • vast measurements and multiverse structures are probably later expansions
  • the Buddha avoids speculative cosmology (e.g. MN 63)

🌞🌌 The few cosmological statements – and why the parallel is remarkable

But the few cosmological things the Buddha said strongly resemble the sun’s future phase as a red giant engulfing the Earth, and also Big Bounce cosmology.

Several scholars have pointed out that it is statistically surprising that precisely those few, probably original, cosmological statements lie so close to modern models when the Buddha hardly spoke about cosmology at all.

If the Buddha merely used random ancient images, why do these particular ones resemble modern astrophysics so closely — while almost no other ancient cosmologies do?

  • Because the Buddha said so little about cosmology, the parallel is remarkable and difficult to explain as pure coincidence.

🌍 Cosmic world-axes in antiquity – ranked by humanism

In many ancient cultures one finds the idea of a cosmic structure (axis mundi) connecting planes of existence vertically – heaven, the human world, and the underworld.

But even though these structures resemble one another surprisingly closely, the traditions differ greatly in how humanistic they are: whether they place human suffering, dignity, and the possibility of liberation at the center – or whether cosmology is used instead to legitimize fate, hierarchy, and power.

For that reason, the most important traditions can be ranked below by degree of humanism.

🥇 Early Buddhism – Sineru / Meru (Nikāya tradition)

Humanism: very high

  • No creator god
  • No eternal soul
  • No absolute rank order
  • All beings can attain liberation
  • Even heavenly worlds are impermanent

The cosmos is not described to explain power, but to show that only Nibbāna is an exit from the whole cycle. This is the most consistently humanistic cosmology of antiquity.

🥈 Early Jesus tradition – “many dwelling places” (Q source, Gospel of Thomas, early Johannine mysticism)

Humanism: high

  • “In my Father’s house are many dwelling places”
  • The kingdom of God as an inner reality
  • No caste or fixed rank order
  • Universal access

The tradition is strongly humanistic at its core, but was later narrowed by church dogma.

🥉 Daoist tradition – Mount Kunlun

Humanism: medium-high

  • No creator god
  • No theology of punishment
  • Harmony and non-violence at the center

But the focus is more individual than universal, and there is no systematic ethics for relieving the suffering of all beings.

⚖️ Shamanic traditions – world-tree / world-pillar

Humanism: medium

  • Healing and balance at the center
  • No absolute god
  • Direct experience

But these traditions are often tribe-specific and lack universal ethics.

🏔️ Classical Hindu tradition – Mount Meru

Humanism: low-medium

  • Caste hierarchy is cosmically legitimized
  • Social inequality is explained karmically
  • Liberation is often reserved for elites

Spiritually deep, but socially problematic.

🌳 Norse tradition – Yggdrasil

Humanism: low

  • Fate is unavoidable
  • No universal exit from suffering
  • Heroism matters more than compassion

The cosmology accepts suffering instead of abolishing it.

🧱 Mesopotamian tradition – ziggurats

Humanism: very low

  • Cosmology legitimizes royal power
  • Human beings are servants
  • Suffering = fate

🔥 Iranian / Zoroastrian tradition – Hara Berezaiti

Humanism: very low

  • Cosmic war between good and evil
  • Human beings are pieces in a metaphysical struggle
  • No universal exit

🏛️ Greek tradition – Olympus / Omphalos

Humanism: very low

  • Gods above human beings
  • Fate governs everything
  • No path of salvation
  • Hierarchy is cosmically legitimized

Olympus represents a strongly hierarchical worldview in which gods stand above human beings and fate governs both gods and mortals. There is no universal path of liberation and no abolition of suffering.

Yet it is precisely in classical Greece that the first real humanism arises – not in mythology, but in philosophy.

In Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, the Stoics, and Epicurus, for the first time an ethics develops in which human reason, dignity, and inner freedom stand above the power of the gods.

Greek civilization is therefore one of the greatest cradles of humanism – precisely because it transcends its own cosmology.

👉 Overall point

The most humanistic traditions arise either:

  • where cosmology is dismantled (Greece)
  • or where cosmology is subordinated to the ending of suffering (Buddhism)

This is a very deep pattern.

The less cosmology is used to legitimize power and hierarchy, the more it is used to relieve suffering and open a universal path to liberation.

Early Buddhism stands almost alone in antiquity in having:

  • a fully developed cosmology
  • without a creator god
  • without hierarchy
  • and with a universal exit from the whole cycle

and therefore appears as the most thoroughly humanistic cosmic tradition of antiquity.

🌌 Plato and the Buddha – two almost contemporary paths to liberation

Around 400 BCE, two of world history’s greatest thinkers appear almost at the same time:

  • the Buddha in India
  • Plato in Greece

They knew nothing of one another, yet independently developed some of the deepest theories about:

  • suffering
  • knowledge
  • soul / consciousness
  • liberation
  • cosmic order

A comparison between them shows both striking similarities and decisive differences.

🧘 🥇 The Buddha – liberation from the whole cosmos

Humanism: very high

  • All conditioned existence is suffering (dukkha)
  • Everything is impermanent (anicca)
  • No eternal soul (anattā)

The goal is not a better place within the cosmos, but an exit from the entire cycle of worlds.

  • No creator god
  • No absolute metaphysical authority
  • All beings can attain liberation
  • Even gods are subject to impermanence
  • The cosmos is secondary – the ending of suffering is primary

Nibbāna is not a heaven, but:

  • the cessation of craving
  • the cessation of rebirth
  • the cessation of cosmic bondage

➡️ The Buddha offers a universal, non-hierarchical path out of the whole system of existence.

🧠 🥈 Plato – the ascent of the soul through knowledge

Humanism: very high (philosophical)

Plato develops a cosmic hierarchy with:

  • the world of the senses (changing, imperfect)
  • the world of Forms (eternal, true, good)

Human beings are souls fallen into the body and must:

  • know the truth
  • purify themselves through philosophy
  • ascend toward the Good
  • The soul is immortal
  • The highest principle is “the Good” (even above the gods)
  • Liberation takes place through knowledge
  • The philosopher can become free already in this life

But:

  • The cosmos remains
  • The soul continues as individual
  • Hierarchy between souls and levels of understanding

➡️ Plato offers an elitist, knowledge-based ascent within the cosmos.

🌟 The deepest similarities (very remarkable)

  • The sensory world is not ultimate reality
  • Ignorance binds the human being
  • Knowledge is liberating
  • A higher level of consciousness is possible
  • An ethical life is necessary for knowledge

Plato: “He who has seen the good will no longer commit injustice.”

The Buddha: “Ignorance is the root of all suffering.”

In practice, both describe:

  • the ascent of consciousness
  • the purification of the mind
  • liberation through insight

⚠️ The decisive differences

Here their paths diverge radically:

🧘 Buddha:

  • No soul
  • No eternal identity
  • No cosmic reward
  • Exit from the whole cosmos
  • Liberation open to all

🧠 Plato:

  • Eternal soul
  • Individual identity is preserved
  • Abiding in the world of Forms
  • The cosmos remains
  • Liberation especially for philosophers

In short:

  • ➡️ The Buddha wants out of the cosmos
  • ➡️ Plato wants upward within the cosmos

🌌 Humanistic assessment

Both are extremely humanistic – but in different ways.

The Buddha:

  • Universal access
  • No elite
  • No god
  • No hierarchy
  • The ending of suffering as the goal

➡️ The most radically humanistic doctrine of liberation in antiquity.

Plato:

  • Reason above the gods
  • Ethics without revelation
  • The dignity of the soul central

But:

  • Hierarchical
  • Elitist
  • The soul remains bound to the cosmos

➡️ Highly humanistic philosophically – but not cosmically radical.

🧭 Summary

  • Creator god – Buddha: ❌ No – Plato: ❌ No
  • Soul – Buddha: ❌ No eternal soul – Plato: ✅ Immortal soul
  • Cosmos – Buddha: Impermanent – Plato: Eternal hierarchy
  • Liberation – Buddha: Out of the whole cosmos – Plato: Up into the world of Forms
  • Access – Buddha: All beings – Plato: Mostly philosophers
  • Humanism – Buddha: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ – Plato: ⭐⭐⭐⭐

🔑 Final point (very strong)

It is almost unbelievable that:

  • two thinkers
  • at opposite ends of the world
  • without contact
  • at the same time

both arrive at the idea:

  • that the human being can be liberated through knowledge
  • that the sensory world is not ultimate reality
  • that ethics and insight are inseparably linked

But while Plato still preserves:

  • the soul
  • hierarchy
  • the cosmos

the Buddha goes one step further:

  • he dissolves the soul itself
  • abolishes the whole cosmos
  • and offers a universal exit from all existence

This makes the Buddha the most radically humanistic cosmic thinker of antiquity – and Plato the most humanistic philosopher in the West.

🧘‍♂️ Sineru, planes of existence, and “transport” (cuti–paṭisandhi)

In early Buddhism it is described that the Buddha and certain monks could move between planes of existence, not as physical journeys through space, but through states of consciousness attained in deep meditation.

The enormous measurements, ring mountains, and cosmic tiers appear only in later cosmological systems.

👉 “In the oldest layers, Sineru is used to show that even the cosmic center of a world-system will one day perish – not because the whole universe ceases, but because all conditioned worlds are subject to impermanence.”

🌌 Cosmic worlds and “transport” between planes of existence in Buddhism

It may perhaps be inferred from early Buddhism that the heavenly planes belonging to this world-system will be destroyed when, in a distant future, the sun engulfs the Earth and this whole cosmic system dissolves.

But Buddhism simultaneously teaches that there are countless other world-systems with their own planes of existence that are not dissolving at the same time.

The question then is how beings are “transported” to these other worlds.

According to the earliest Buddhist texts, there is no physical journey through space and time. The transition occurs through rebirth by means of consciousness and karma.

  • When a being dies (cuti), a new consciousness (paṭisandhi) immediately arises in another plane of existence, determined by the state of mind and previous actions.
  • No intermediate journey and no movement through space are described – only an instantaneous continuity of consciousness, like one flame lighting a new flame.
  • Thus beings leave this world-system long before its physical dissolution and are reborn in other worlds in accordance with their karma.

🧠 Consciousness and the brain as a possible “antenna” (hypothesis)

I also think it cannot be ruled out that there are other real planes of existence, and that the brain may in a certain sense function like an antenna receiving information from them.

Perhaps this happens through extremely small quantum events in strong field gradients over extremely short distances, of the kind found everywhere in the brain. If so, consciousness is not only something created locally in neurons, but also something that, to a limited extent, can receive information from a larger cosmic or non-local level.

One possible motivation for this thought is that both animals and humans solve complex open-ended problems every day that no one has yet taught artificial intelligence to solve in the same way without such an “antenna.”

Examples are:

  • driving a car safely from A to B in unpredictable traffic situations
  • using hands and arms to fold clothes neatly
  • grasping objects with precise force and timing
  • navigating intuitively in complex environments

These tasks require:

  • continuous sensing
  • rapid holistic understanding
  • bodily coordination
  • flexible problem-solving

and are often solved without conscious calculation.

It is therefore tempting to speculate that consciousness is not exclusively a closed system in the brain, but may also have access to a broader field of information not yet fully understood by neuroscience or artificial intelligence.

🟢 The earliest ideas about planes of existence in Buddhism

According to modern scholars, the early Buddha taught that consciousness can be reborn in many different forms of existence – human beings, animals, heavenly worlds, and worlds of suffering. These worlds were understood as real, but not eternal. No god judges – karma and the direction of the mind determine rebirth.

Even the highest heavenly worlds are impermanent. Therefore the Buddha pointed to one exit lying beyond all worlds: Nibbāna.

🔹 TEXTUAL CLARIFICATION – why beings are already reborn before Sineru burns up 🔹

In the very earliest Buddhist texts (the Nikāya canon), world-dissolution (saṃvaṭṭa) is described as a slow cosmic process, not as a sudden catastrophe.

🔥 What is described – and what is not?

📜 Aṅguttara Nikāya 7.62 – Sattasūriya Sutta (The Seven Suns)

It says:

“Ayaṃ mahāpathavī ca Sineru ca pabbatarājā ādittā bhavissanti, sampajjalitā, ekā jalitā bhavissanti.”

• “This great earth and Sineru, king of mountains, will be set ablaze, flare up, and become one burning mass.” 🔥

And further:

“Seyyathāpi sappi vā telaṃ vā anupādisesaṃ pariyādāya jhāyeyya…”

• “Just as clarified butter or oil burns completely without ash or soot.”

➡️ Crucial observation:

  • ❌ Nothing is said about sattā (beings) dying, pāṇa (living creatures) perishing, or maraṇa (death) of humans, animals, or gods.

📌 In the Pāli texts, the death of beings is always mentioned explicitly when relevant. 👉 That it is not mentioned here is therefore meaningful.

🧠 The text-based conclusion

  • When sattā (beings) are not mentioned while the entire physical structure is destroyed,
  • this means, according to close reading and modern scholarship:
  • ➡️ there are already no beings left in this world-system.

That is:

  • punabbhava (rebirth) has already ceased locally
  • beings have already died in their previous lives
  • and have been reborn elsewhere in accordance with kamma (karma)

🔄 World-dissolution as process – not total annihilation

📜 Dīgha Nikāya 1 – Brahmajāla Sutta

“Saṃvaṭṭati loko, vivaṭṭati loko.”

• “The world contracts, the world expands.”

  • No elemental mechanics are mentioned
  • No total annihilation of all life
  • Only the gradual ending of conditions

🌌 Other worlds exist simultaneously

📜 Majjhima Nikāya 49 – Brahma-nimantanika Sutta

“Atthi kho, brahme, añño loko.”

• “There are other worlds, Brahmā.”

  • When one cakkavāḷa (world-system) ceases:
  • others still exist
  • beings are reborn there
  • not through physical movement
  • but through karmic rebirth (cuti–paṭisandhi)

🔄 Cosmic cycles without the end of life

📜 Majjhima Nikāya 4 – Bhayabherava Sutta

“Anekepi saṃvaṭṭavivaṭṭakappe anussarāmi.”

• “I recollect many aeons of world-contraction and expansion.”

  • Beings are reborn across these cycles
  • No cosmic pause is described in which all life ceases

🔥 Only fire in the earliest Buddhism

  • In the Nikāya texts there is only: 🔥 destruction by fire (tejo-saṃvaṭṭa)
  • ❌ No udaka-saṃvaṭṭa (water)
  • ❌ No vāta-saṃvaṭṭa (wind)

Water and wind appear only in later Abhidhamma and commentary traditions.

🧭 The decisive distinction

  • 🔥 The destruction of Sineru: one local cakkavāḷa ceases
  • 🌌 Not the whole universe
  • 🧠 Sattā are already reborn elsewhere
  • 🚫 No physical “evacuation”
  • ✅ Only kamma and the direction of the mind determine “where to”

🕊️ Only Nibbāna (nibbānaṃ) is the cessation of the whole cycle (saṃsāra).

✨ In short

  • That the death of beings is not mentioned means: 👉 they are already reborn elsewhere (punabbhava)
  • Sineru burns afterward 🔥
  • Life has already left the system 🌌

This is directly text-based, Pāli-documented, and consistent with modern scholarship on the very earliest Buddhism.

🌌 World-destructions in the earliest Buddhist texts – and modern cosmology

According to the earliest Buddhist scriptures (the Pāli canon), worlds perish in great cycles of contraction (samvatta).

  • Oldest and most original mention: destruction by fire.
  • Later expansions (still early, but not original): destruction by water and wind.
  • The heavens above the affected level always survive.

According to the cosmic teaching (AN 7.62 and other texts):

  • Fire → the world perishes up to the Ābhassara heaven.

☀️ The seven suns (Sattasūriya Sutta, AN 7.62)

  • Small streams dry up.
  • Larger rivers dry up.
  • Lakes dry up.
  • Even the Ganges dries up.
  • The oceans evaporate.
  • The earth begins to glow and smoke.
  • The earth and Sineru burst into flames and are consumed without remainder.

The sutta describes only the dissolution of the physical world in this one world-system.

No death of beings is mentioned, which in the early Buddhist context means that beings have already been reborn elsewhere in accordance with their karma.

The heavens are not mentioned in this sutta. It therefore does not describe the total annihilation of all planes of existence, but the dissolution of the physical world-structure and the planes of existence belonging to this one world-system.

This can be understood figuratively: not seven suns, but the swelling of the sun — fully in line with modern astronomy regarding the sun’s development into a red giant.

🌍 Cosmological background + Mahākappa (4 phases)

The universe goes through cycles of:

  • expansion/unfolding (vivatta)
  • continuance
  • contraction/destruction (samvatta)
  • an empty period before a new unfolding

There are:

  • thousandfold world-systems
  • thousand-times-thousandfold systems
  • and “great thousandfold world-systems”

A full cycle is called a mahākappa.

The Buddha said: if one had a mountain 14 km high and stroked it with a silk cloth once every 100 years, the mountain would wear away faster than a mahākappa would pass.

🌌 1. What is a mahākappa?

In early Buddhism, a mahākappa (great aeon) is the longest conceivable cosmic unit of time.

A mahākappa consists of four immense periods which together make up one complete cosmic cycle:

  • samvaṭṭa – world dissolution (contraction / destruction)
  • samvaṭṭa-ṭṭhāyin – empty intermediate period after dissolution
  • vivaṭṭa – world re-arising / unfolding (expansion)
  • vivaṭṭa-ṭṭhāyin – stable period in which the world endures

So: 👉 One mahākappa = dissolution → emptiness → new unfolding → stable existence.

Not just one contraction and one expansion – but also:

  • a long empty phase without a physical world
  • and a long stable phase afterwards

🧭 2. Is this one universe or many?

Very important:

  • A mahākappa applies to one world-system (one cakkavāḷa).

But there are:

  • countless world-systems
  • which are not necessarily in the same phase at the same time

So:

  • 👉 When one world is dissolving
  • 👉 others are in a stable phase
  • 👉 and others in new unfolding

It is a genuine multiverse system.

🔥 3. World-dissolution is not one single event

In the texts, samvaṭṭa (dissolution) is described as:

  • extremely slow
  • stepwise
  • taking place over unimaginably long periods

And in later tradition it can occur in three ways:

  • by fire (tejo-saṃvaṭṭa)
  • by water (udaka-saṃvaṭṭa)
  • by wind (vāta-saṃvaṭṭa)

But in the oldest layers only fire is mentioned. This means: 👉 dissolution is not one catastrophe, but a long cosmic process.

🌌 4. Relation to Big Bounce cosmology

Big Bounce models typically have:

  • expansion
  • contraction
  • bounce
  • new expansion

But Buddhism has a richer structure:

  1. stable world (vivaṭṭa-ṭṭhāyin)
  2. dissolution (samvaṭṭa)
  3. empty phase without world (samvaṭṭa-ṭṭhāyin)
  4. new unfolding (vivaṭṭa)

→ then again a stable phase

So: 👉 not just bounce, but also a long “cosmic emptiness” between universes.

This resembles modern ideas such as:

  • “ekpyrotic phase”
  • “pre-big-bang vacuum”
  • “quantum vacuum state”

🧠 5. The Buddha directly says he experienced many mahākappas

In e.g. MN 4 and MN 36 the Buddha says:

“I recollected many aeons of world-contraction, many aeons of world-expansion, many aeons of both movements…”

This is extremely important: he does not merely say many lives, but many cosmic cycles.

Thus: 👉 Saṃsāra extends across countless universes through many mahākappas.

Scientific simulations show:

  • Inflation often creates inhomogeneous universes.
  • Contraction (cosmic contraction) makes the universe more homogeneous, flat, and isotropic.

This fits surprisingly well with Buddhism’s teaching that universes unfold after contraction from a subtle, harmonious state.

I recollected my many previous lives, namely: one birth, two births, three births, four births, five births, ten births, twenty births, thirty births, forty births, fifty births, one hundred births, one thousand births, one hundred thousand births, many aeons in which the world slowly contracts, many aeons in which the world slowly unfolds again, many aeons in which these two movements follow one another through inconceivably long cosmic cycles...

‘There I was with such a name, of such a clan, with such an appearance; this was my food, such was my experience of pleasure and pain, such was my lifespan; and dying there, I arose elsewhere; and there again I was with such a name, of such a clan, with such an appearance; this was my food, such was my experience of pleasure and pain, such was my lifespan; and dying there, I arose here.’

Thus I recollected my many previous lives with their forms and details...

This was the first true knowledge I obtained in the first watch of the night...

Then I directed my mind to the knowledge of the passing away and reappearance of beings... I saw beings dying and reappearing, inferior and superior, beautiful and ugly, fortunate and unfortunate, and I understood how beings move on in accordance with their actions...

Then I directed my mind to the knowledge of the destruction of the taints. I directly knew: ‘This is suffering.’ ‘This is the origin of suffering.’ ‘This is the cessation of suffering.’ ‘This is the path leading to the cessation of suffering.’ ...

I directly knew: ‘Birth is ended, the holy life fulfilled, what had to be done has been done; there is no more return to any state of being.’ ...

It is because I see two benefits that I still seek secluded resting places in the forest thickets: I see a pleasant abiding for myself here and now, and I have compassion for future generations.”

👼 Life in the heavens + the “fall” into a new world (vivatta)

👼 Life in the heavens is far better than life on Earth

The higher heavens (Ābhassara, Subhakiṇṇa, Vehapphala, and the arūpa worlds) are described as places with:

  • no disease
  • no hunger
  • subtle or immaterial bodies
  • jhāna-bliss
  • enormously long lives
  • no conflicts
  • great degrees of peace, light, and clarity

The Buddha says that even the highest earthly happiness cannot compare.

🔥 The fate of beings in the fire-destruction during cosmic contraction

  • Humans, animals, hell beings, and lower gods perish together with their realm.
  • Beings up to Ābhassara can ascend to higher heavens or be reborn later.
  • The highest heavens (above Vehapphala, including the arūpa worlds) always survive.
  • Since there are countless world-systems, beings can be reborn in other universes that are not dissolving.

🌱 The beginning of a new world (vivatta) – the “fall”

When a world unfolds:

  • The radiant Ābhassara devas come first.
  • They are self-luminous, sexless, without coarse bodies, without need of food.
  • Some taste the earth’s nourishment → lose their radiance → their bodies become heavier.
  • Differences arise → pride, envy, conflict.

This is the “fall” described in the Aggañña Sutta.

The Buddha said:

  • “This consciousness is luminous, but defiled by adventitious impurities.” (AN 1.49–52)

The nature of mind is luminous even when universes perish.

No eternal soul — but karmic continuity:

  • like a flame lighting a new flame
  • like waves that arise and die while the depth of the sea remains

🧘 Conclusion

Everything conditioned falls away:

  • worlds
  • heavens
  • gods
  • beings

Both Buddhism and modern cosmology describe cycles of:

  • expansion
  • continuance
  • contraction
  • new unfolding

Modern physics suggests that contraction makes the universe homogeneous — almost like Buddhism’s doctrine of samvatta.

The Earth will burn when the sun becomes a red giant.

But because there is the unconditioned, and because the mind is luminous, beings can realize that which never perishes — and become liberated from the very cycle of worlds arising and passing away.

🔹 The purpose of an experiment 🔹

I am conducting this experiment to challenge a view that I experience as both reductionist and ultimately destructive: the idea that the human being is nothing more than the physical brain.

If this view were fully adequate, then:

  • conscious intention should not be able to affect physical randomness
  • human will should be wholly derived from deterministic or random processes
  • subjective experience should be a mere by-product without causal force

My experiment is therefore an attempt to investigate whether consciousness can have an independent, measurable influence on physical processes — even when these are based on electronic noise and quantum fluctuations.

It is not about “proving” anything definitively, but about testing whether reality behaves less mechanically than a purely materialist worldview assumes.

A PEAR Lab–inspired experiment on consciousness, randomness, and physical noise

Inspired by the pioneering experiments at the PEAR laboratory at Princeton University and related research, this project investigates a fundamental question: Can human consciousness influence physical systems in a measurable way?

To investigate this, I have built a hardware-based noise generator that exploits physical random processes. The system is designed and assembled on a custom-made printed circuit board and placed in an enclosure. It continuously generates physical noise, which is processed and analyzed in real time.

On the software side, I have developed custom tools in Python for data collection, live visualization, and statistical analysis. The aim is to test whether focused human intention can lead to deviations from randomness that exceed what one would statistically expect from pure chance.

The project assumes no predetermined outcome. Instead, standardized statistical methods, controlled measurements, and transparent data logging are used to investigate whether reproducible and statistically significant effects can be observed.

What you see here is the current status of the experiment — hardware, software, live data, and ongoing development.

  • A PEAR Lab–Inspired Experiment on Mind, Randomness, and Physical Noise
  • Program files / OneDrive folder

It is difficult to imagine how one could influence quantum events in an electronic circuit when one does not know what is happening moment by moment in voltages and currents. But who knows what a presence one has on other planes of existence might be able to do, or what others there might hypothetically do.

Perhaps we are already steering quantum processes in the brain every day when we make decisions, solve open-ended problems, and do things that AI still cannot do – for example, driving a car safely from A to B.

To obtain faster and statistically stronger measurements, one might perhaps use many independent zener-noise generators and analyze their combined output. But it takes time to build that many circuits, especially when one also has a physically demanding full-time job.

Perhaps it makes the most sense simply to continue with the setup I already have – and try to capture something resembling what the PEAR Lab at Princeton said they observed. It just takes longer to gather enough data.

What I experienced actually resembles something PEAR-LAB also noticed.

⭐ What PEAR-LAB found in their 30-year noise experiments

Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research (PEAR) conducted experiments for nearly three decades in which ordinary people tried to influence random noise generators by intention alone.

The most striking thing was that the noise generators responded – but not always in the way the subject wanted.

Intention reversal

In many tests something strange happened:

  • When the operator tried to make the curve go up, it sometimes went down
  • When the person tried to make it go down, it went up
  • The effect did not look like simple noise – there were patterns, directions, and correlations

The researchers gave it a name: intention reversal – a phenomenon in which the system responds, but in the opposite direction from the conscious intention.

The effect was weak, but statistically strong

A single session rarely showed anything dramatic. But when thousands of sessions were pooled, the deviation was consistent and so statistically strong that the probability against chance was calculated as less than one in trillions.

Not pure noise – but a pattern

PEAR found effects such as:

  • autocorrelations in the noise
  • deviations that did not follow the normal distribution
  • connections between the operator’s mental state and the results
  • experimenter effects (the person setting up the experiment influenced the outcome)

It behaved too “systematically” for pure chance to explain it.

Personal signatures

  • Some people always pulled the curve slightly upward
  • Some always slightly downward
  • And some had a stable tendency toward intention reversal

It was as if each person had a kind of “psychic signature” in the noise.

Distance and time made no difference

  • The operator was in another building
  • In another state
  • On the other side of the USA
  • Or even in experiments where the intention was supplied after the data had been generated (retro-experiments)

No simple physical explanation

The setups were run in Faraday cages, electromagnetically shielded, tested against control sessions, and across different kinds of noise generators – but nothing technical could explain the patterns.

PEAR’s cautious conclusion was:

  • There exists a form of systematic connection between human intention and physical randomness that cannot easily be explained on the basis of current physics or psychology.

What I myself saw in my measurements resembles a little what PEAR-LAB found: a kind of response – just not necessarily in the direction one consciously tries to produce.

And that makes it very fascinating to investigate further.

🔹 Can the present change the past – without us noticing it?

Some of the most controversial results from PEAR-LAB at Princeton pointed toward what is called retrocausality: that intention in the present can influence the statistics in data that have already been generated in the past.

It sounds like science fiction, but in several of their so-called retro-PK experiments such effects did appear:

👉 The statistical distribution of earlier bits changed slightly, but consistently.

If the past can really be influenced, should we not then experience time paradoxes?

For example:

  • 1 bit in the past → triggers a lightning strike
  • 0 bit → no lightning strike

A lightning strike can change the weather → human behavior → who meets whom → who is born → DNA → the whole future — a classic butterfly effect.

And yet we never experience our present being “rewritten.” Why?

⭐ Three of the most common interpretations

✔ 1) Self-consistency (the Novikov principle)

The most widespread interpretation is that the universe only permits retrocausal effects that do not create logical paradoxes.

  • either the change does not happen
  • or its consequences are neutralized
  • or the effect remains statistically microscopic

The past can therefore only be “bent” within limits that preserve the existing timeline.

This fits well with PEAR-LAB’s observations: small, systematic deviations — but never macroscopic paradoxes.

✔ 2) Many-Worlds / time-branches

In Everett’s many-worlds interpretation, every quantum possibility creates a new branch of reality.

  • the change is realized in another branch
  • the original branch remains unchanged

In the alternative branch:

  • the lightning strikes
  • the weather changes
  • history develops differently

But you yourself experience only the branch in which your consciousness already exists. Therefore you never register that anything “switches.”

✔ 3) No actual change of the past – only statistical shifts

A more conservative interpretation is that no concrete past events are changed, but there arise only:

  • weak correlations
  • biases in probability distributions
  • non-local connections without information paradoxes

In this interpretation, retrocausality is not a rewriting of history, but a time-symmetric coupling in which:

  • present and past enter into one single quantum description
  • without any classical causal chain being broken

This is close to ideas found in Aharonov, Cramer, and Huw Price.

⭐ Brief conclusion

PEAR-LAB’s data may be interpreted as signs that:

  • the present and the past are not completely independent
  • but the connection is strongly constrained

Whatever interpretation one chooses, the following hold:

  • ✔ The effects are small
  • ✔ No macroscopic paradoxes arise
  • ✔ Experienced reality remains consistent

The past may possibly be influenced statistically, but never in ways that break the coherent reality we experience.

🔹 My own provisional interpretation

Personally, I lean most toward interpretation 3 — namely that there are no concrete rewritings of the past, but rather time-symmetric statistical shifts, where present and past enter into one single quantum description.

However, with one possible extension:

👉 That at certain free-choice events, a limited number of temporary time-branches may arise, diverging briefly — but merging again when the consequences remain within a self-consistent framework.

In this model:

  • Free choices open a small outcome space
  • Several quantum possibilities may be realized temporarily
  • The branches do not exist permanently as in classical many-worlds
  • Only the courses that are mutually consistent survive and “recombine”

This avoids both:

  • classical time paradoxes
  • infinitely branching worlds
  • and the need for macroscopic rewriting of history

At the same time, it preserves the possibility that consciousness and intention play an active role in which outcomes become stabilized.

This approach lies somewhere between:

  • Aharonov’s time-symmetric quantum mechanics
  • Cramer’s transactional interpretation
  • and a strongly limited, recombining variant of many-worlds

At the same time, it is worth noting that all of these well-known interpretations are probably incomplete, in that they describe only phenomena within the observable physical plane and do not take into account what may possibly occur in other layers or planes of existence of reality, if such exist.

— and taken together, this model seems to me to fit best with both PEAR-LAB’s observations and the complete absence of experienced time paradoxes in everyday life.
